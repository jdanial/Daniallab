Research overview
We develop new instruments, software, and assays to push single molecule microscopy towards structural imaging of living human samples.
What drives us
How far can we push single molecule imaging
Single molecule microscopy has become an indispensable tool in biological research. Owing to the pioneering work of our predecessors, we can now track and locate molecules, one-at-a-time, inside cells, and at high precision. This has allowed us to study the anomalous behaviour of single proteins inside their natural habitat and to detect early molecular events that are key to cellular life. We are curious how to push single molecule imaging to its limits. Among the questions we ask ourselves, can we image the structure of flexible protein assemblies, one-at-a-time, and at a single epitope level? Can we use these tools to study fatal human disorders and tightly regulated cellular processes that rely on structurally heterogenous protein assemblies one assembly at a time, at Angstrom resolution, and in human-relevant samples? How far can we democratise these tools; can individual labs, or even individuals, in low-resource settings have access to such technology?  
Instrumentation
We build cutting-edge, single-molecule microscopes to push the frontiers of structural imaging
We have previously developed the NanoPro, an economic, open-source, and high-throughput single molecule localisation microscope that delivers median localisation precisions less than 2 nanometres. We are now developing the next-generation of NanoPro, an ultra-stable and ultra-economic microscope that delivers nanometre-to-Angstrom image resolutions to image the ultra-structure of single proteins and protein assemblies.

Software
We develop open-source software to map the shape of flexible proteins at an Angstrom scale.
We have previously developed the Automated Structures Analysis Program (ASAP) to map the ultra-structure of structured macromolecular complexes with Angstrom accuracy using machine learning and single particle averaging. Since then, we developed a string of computational tools for single molecule microscopy for various applications. We are now focused on developing computational tools that can capture the heterogeneity of single proteins and protein assemblies at and below the diffraction limit.

Application
We apply these tools to study developmental, immunological, and neurodegenerative disorders at a structural level.
Taking neurodegeneration as an example, we use our tools to image the assorted, nanoscopic, and scarce aggregates of the tau, alpha-synuclein, and amyloid beta proteins in human (living and post-mortem brains as well as peripheral organs) and human-derived (stem cells and organoids) samples of proteinopathies (Alzheimer's / Parkinson's diseases and Down Syndrome) to study the molecular basis of neurodevelopment and degeneration. We are currently studying these diseases - and other cellular processes - at a single molecule, (ultra)-structural level to ultimately understand their structural mechanisms.
